Lessons from Software

The Software exhibition was curated by Jack Burnham for the Jewish Museum in 1970. The exhibition was first-of-its-kind in many regards, but its reception has been clouded over by a discourse on its ''failures.'' Unsurprisingly, the reception of failure has clouded over aspects of the exhibition that provide models for curating exhibitions of work in all media. This list is a partial remedy to the exhibition's current reception. Some lessons were implemented imperfectly for the Software exhibition, but they can be adapted more easily in the 2020s.

Lessons to follow from Software.

  1. Hire a technical advisor to remain on-site prior, during, and after the opening of the exhibition.
  2. If popular works are crowded, give visitors something to do while waiting in line.
  3. Keep the museum open until 11:00 p.m.
  4. Reuse materials and equipment. Give artists first dibs on purchasing equipment used in the exhibition.
  5. Give artists first dibs on purchasing discounted equipment after it was used in the exhibition.
  6. For artists with custom-made equipment, consider including a ''Portable repair kit'' with your work, in case any problems arise during the run of the exhibition.
  7. Give your artists ample installation time: over a month, if necessary.
  8. Show both the software and hardware.
  9. Make sure to hire extra staff -- not unpaid volunteers -- to supervise each interactive work.
